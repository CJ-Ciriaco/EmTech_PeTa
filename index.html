Academic Work

Our Research

Effectiveness of Atmospheric Humidity Harvesting for Urban Agriculture

Water scarcity continues to challenge agricultural productivity in high-elevation and urban areas of the Philippines, where access to traditional water sources is limited. This study evaluates the viability of dehumidifier-based Atmospheric Water Harvesting (AWH) systems as an alternative irrigation source, comparing performance across two contrasting urban environments — Marikina City (low-elevation urban core) and Tarlac City (higher-elevation peri-urban area) — using Mung Bean (Vigna radiata) growth trials over a four-week hydroponic experiment.

⚠️

Problem Statement

No study has directly compared dehumidifier-based AWH systems between high- and low-elevation urban environments in the Philippines. This gap leaves policymakers and communities without empirical data on whether elevation or urban density more significantly affects water yield and quality for agriculture.

🎯

Research Questions

How viable is atmospheric humidity harvesting for irrigation in humid Philippine regions? The study specifically measures: (a) water quality via pH, (b) agricultural suitability via plant growth, and (c) efficiency via consistency of water collection across Marikina and Tarlac.

🔬

Methodology

Quantitative experimental design with 15 Mung Bean plants across three groups — 6 in Marikina, 6 in Tarlac, and 3 indoor controls. Daily water volume, pH, plant height, and leaf count were recorded over 4 weeks. Statistical analyses: One-Way ANOVA (Welch's), Kruskal-Wallis, Shapiro-Wilk, and Mann-Whitney U tests using Jamovi software.

Research Hypotheses

The researchers hypothesize that (1) atmospheric water pH will exhibit significantly higher alkalinity than the levels below 5.6 typically observed in Metro Manila rainwater; (2) adjusting harvested water to approximately pH 6.37 will result in significantly greater biomass and leaf development in Mung Beans compared to non-adjusted sources; and (3) water collection efficiency will be significantly higher in Tarlac than in Marikina, as harvesting volumes vary based on elevation, temperature, and air pressure.

Plant Height — Not Statistically Significant

By week 4, the control group (tap water) achieved the highest mean height of 18.83 cm, followed by Tarlac AWH at 10.50 cm and Marikina AWH at 7.75 cm. However, the Kruskal-Wallis test (χ² = 4.37, p = 0.113) confirmed no statistically significant difference among groups, suggesting atmospheric water does not conclusively hinder Mung Bean vertical growth.

2
Leaf Count — Not Statistically Significant

The control group led with a mean of 4.33 leaves, Tarlac AWH averaged 2.50, and Marikina AWH 1.50. The One-Way ANOVA (Welch's F = 1.34, p = 0.341) and Shapiro-Wilk normality test (W = 0.929, p = 0.260) confirmed normal distribution and no statistically significant difference — supporting AWH as a viable supplementary irrigation source.

3
Water Volume — No Significant Difference Between Sites

Both sites produced comparable daily water volumes. Tarlac averaged 14.3 mL/day versus Marikina's 13.9 mL/day, but Marikina showed lower variability (SD 6.90 vs. 6.95). The Mann-Whitney U test (U = 377, p = 0.806) confirmed no statistically significant difference, indicating that elevation did not meaningfully affect quantity harvested.

4
Water pH — Statistically Significant Difference

pH levels differed significantly between sites (Welch's t = 2.74, df = 47.1, p = 0.009). Marikina water was slightly more alkaline (mean pH 6.58) and more consistent than Tarlac (mean pH 6.45), reflecting the influence of local environmental factors. Both values fall within the ideal agricultural pH range of 6.0–7.5.

Chapter 3 — Methodology

Experimental Setup

Materials & Equipment

Dehumidifier (primary AWH unit), water pump, PVC pipe frame, dog cage matting, cotton filter, collection pail, hydroponic pots (×15), Mung Bean seeds, potting soil, pH water strips, and a beaker for precise measurement. Total estimated cost: ₱7,325.00.

Sites & Duration

Experiment ran for 4 weeks at two locations: Marikina City (low-elevation urban core, Urban Heat Island effect) and Tarlac City (higher-elevation peri-urban). A 3-plant indoor control group received 5 mL of standard tap water daily for baseline comparison.

Measurement Protocol

Daily water yield was measured by graduated cylinder at a fixed time. pH and TDS were tested before and after acidification to target pH 6.37. Plant height and leaf count were recorded every 48 hours. Temperature and relative humidity were logged three times daily (morning, noon, evening) via digital hygrometer.

pH Adjustment

Diluted sulfuric acid was added incrementally to harvested water to neutralize alkalinity and achieve the target irrigation pH of ~6.37, following Albano et al. (2017). This mediating step ensures pH-adjusted water is consistent across trials and does not introduce nutritional deficiencies or toxicities in the hydroponic substrate.

Chapter 5 — Conclusions

Conclusions & Recommendations

Overall Conclusion

Atmospheric humidity harvesting systems proved capable of producing usable irrigation water in both urban settings, with no statistically significant difference in volume between Marikina and Tarlac. While plant growth outcomes numerically favored the tap-water control, the differences were not statistically significant — establishing AWH as a viable supplementary irrigation source. The only statistically significant finding was in water pH, with Marikina producing slightly more alkaline and more consistent water than Tarlac, reflecting the influence of local environmental conditions. AWH is not yet a full replacement for conventional sources, but demonstrates strong potential as a sustainable, decentralized option for small-scale urban agriculture in humid Philippine regions.

Why It Matters

Significance of the Study

1
Residential Communities

Provides a practical, low-cost alternative water source for home gardening and small-scale food production in water-scarce areas, improving household food security and reducing dependence on costly or unreliable water systems.

2
Local Government Units (LGUs)

Offers an innovative, cost-effective approach to addressing water scarcity in upland and drought-prone communities. Findings can inform sustainable water management policies and integration of AWH into disaster risk reduction and climate adaptation programs.

3
National Government & NGOs

Supports evidence-based policies in sustainable agriculture and water conservation. NGOs focused on rural development and climate action may use this study to implement community-based AWH initiatives and strengthen resilience in water-stressed regions.

4
Future Researchers

Establishes empirical data on AWH in Philippine urban environments. Future work should explore improved condensation materials (e.g., nano-coated or hydrophilic surfaces), different crop varieties, longer experimental periods, and integration with automated hydroponic systems to optimize yield and sustainability.
